Allison Gopnik, a neuroscientist and PI at the [[Whitehead Institute]], presented her lab's research on the neurological basis of spontaneous, self-initiated movement as a proxy for free will. Her key finding is that dopamine neurons exhibit a ramping activity pattern before self-initiated movements in mice: the ramp's rate determines timing, and its amplitude at the moment of action is approximately threshold-level. Critically, the process is genuinely probabilistic rather than deterministic — high dopamine activity raises the probability of movement without fixing the exact moment.

### 2026-05-07 — Dopamine ramp theory of free will
<!-- source_hash: c1cbf444f00990cabbd78769085e004dcb463b48400c2320903980ec92011096 -->
Allison Gopnik, a neuroscientist at the Whitehead Institute, presented her lab's research operationalizing free will as self-initiated movement — distinguished from reflexive reactions to external stimuli. Using fiber photometry in mice trained on a self-timed movement task, her lab found that dopaminergic neuron activity ramps up from a cue toward a threshold that triggers movement; the ramp rate determines movement timing, and baseline dopamine tone before any cue carries predictive information about when the animal will act. She proposed that this probabilistic, threshold-based mechanism is the brain's internal 'fire' for spontaneous action, with implications for Parkinson's disease (insufficient ramp) and mania or addiction (excessive or erratic ramping).
