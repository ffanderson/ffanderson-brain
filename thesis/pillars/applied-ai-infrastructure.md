## Definition

Picks-and-shovels infrastructure for the AI stack — specifically
human-feedback data pipelines (RLHF/RLBF), annotation tooling,
specialised training-data marketplaces, evaluation infrastructure, and
the operational layer underneath frontier labs. The bet is that as
foundation models commoditise, the durable margin migrates to the
companies that own the data flywheel and the evaluation harness.

## Why now (if at all)

- Foundation-model providers are increasingly buying or building their
  own data infrastructure; the gap between what labs need and what
  exists is real and currently unfilled.
- Several operators in the network ([[Brian]], [[Badri]], [[Mercor]])
  are independently pivoting in this direction.
- The OpenAI / Anthropic / Scale AI corridor is being actively
  cultivated as a referral network for spinout founders.

## Investment criteria

What this thesis would bet on (if articulated):
- Technical founders with applied-ML pedigree (Meta RL, Scale AI,
  OpenAI, Anthropic, MosaicML alumni profiles)
- A specific data flywheel or evaluation moat — not a "we'll build a
  better data labeling tool" pitch
- Direct relationships with the labs as customers
- A defensible thesis on what doesn't get commoditised when models do

## What we'd pass on

- Data labeling 2.0 without a clear specialised vertical
- Companies whose customer base is "the labs" with no signed contracts
- Tools that any lab can replicate in 90 days

## Tension with the vertical-agents pillar

This is a fundamentally different bet — slower margins, longer build
cycles, fewer winners, deeper technical moats. It does not contradict
the vertical-agents pillar but it is not a complement either; it
competes for attention and capital.

The honest reading of the corpus right now: this isn't a deliberate
pillar yet — it's a cluster of conversations that look adjacent because
the operators happen to come from AI-lab pedigrees. The owner should
decide: deliberate position, or coincidence to walk away from?
